# Core Concepts

## Space
Space is the three-dimensional, continuous, and infinitely extended framework in which the positions of particles can be specified at any instant.

Properties:
- **Absolute:** Properties of space are independent of the objects within it.
- **Three-dimensional:** Every point can be specified by three coordinates.
- **Euclidean Geometry:** Distances follow the Euclidean metric.
- **Continuity:** Space is continuous. Therefore, between any two points there are infinitely many intermediate points.
- **Homogeneity:** All locations in space are equivalent. The laws of physics do not depend on absolute position.
- **Isotropy:** Space has no preferred direction. Physical laws are identical in every direction.

## Time
Time is a continuous scalar quantity that flows uniformly and independently of all material objects, providing an ordering of events.

Properties:
- **Absolute:** Time passes identically for all observers.
- **Independent:** Time is separate from spatial coordinates.
- **One-dimensional:** Events are ordered by a single parameter $t$.
- **Continuity:** Time is continuous and has no smallest interval.
- **Uniformity:** Equal intervals of time are identical everywhere.
- **Homogeneity:** Physical laws do not depend on the absolute moment in time.
- **Global Simultaneity:** If two events occur at the same time for one observer, they do so for all observers.

## Mass
Mass is an intrinsic, positive scalar property of a body that quantifies its resistance to acceleration under the action of forces.

Properties:
- **Inertia:** Mass measures resistance to acceleration.
- **Intrinsic:** Mass belongs to the object itself.
- **Additivity:** Total mass of a system equals the sum of its parts.
- **Positivity:** Mass is always positive.
- **Gravitational Equivalence:** In Newtonian gravitation, inertial mass is the same as gravitational mass.

## Mechanical System
A mechanical system is a set of classical particles or rigid bodies whose motion is described by the laws of classical mechanics, together with specified constraints and forces acting on them.

Properties:
- **Configuration Space:** A mechanical system must have a configuration, meaning the spatial arrangement of its parts. Configuration space is the set of all possible configurations the system can have.
- **Mechanical State:** A mechanical system must have a state, which is a point in configuration space characterized by the positions and momenta of its constituent parts.
- **Dynamical Law:** A mechanical system must have a law that governs the evolution of the system.

## Classical Particle
A classical particle is an idealized object that has a definite position and mass but no spatial extent or internal structure, so that its size, shape, and rotation can be neglected when analyzing its motion.

A classical particle is essentially a point-like particle.

Properties:
- **Position:** At any given time, the particle's location in space can be specified by coordinates.
- **Massive:** The particle has mass, which gives inertia to the particle.
- **Non-volumetric:** The particle has no volume or spatial extent.
- **Non-orientable:** The particle has no intrinsic orientation.
- **Non-rotatory:** The particle has no spin angular momentum (but it can have orbital angular momentum)

## Rigid Body
A rigid body is an idealized object consisting of a system of classical particles, in which the distance between any two constituent particles remains constant in time, regardless of external forces or torques acting on it.

Properties:
- **Center of mass:** The rigid body has a hypothetical point where the entire mass of the body may be assumed to be concentrated to visualize its motion.
- **Position:** At any given time, the rigid body’s location in space is defined by the location of its center of mass, which can be specified by coordinates. This defines the 3 translational degrees of freedom for the rigid body.
- **Massive:** The rigid body has mass, which gives inertia to the rigid body. The mass of a rigid body is defined as the sum of the masses of all its constituent classical particles.
- **Volumetric:** The rigid body has defined volume or spatial extent. Therefore, it has a definite shape and size.
- **Moment of Inertia:** Combined with its shape, the mass distribution of the rigid body determines its moment of inertia, which controls how the body rotates under applied torques.
- **Orientable:** The rigid body has a orientation in space. The orientation defines the 3 rotational degrees of freedom.
- **Rotatory:** The rigid body has spin angular momentum (but not necessarily orbital angular momentum). Therefore, it can rotate about any of its constituent particles.
- **Non-deformable:** The shape of the rigid body is fixed and the body cannot be deformed. The concepts of stress and strain does not apply to a rigid body.
